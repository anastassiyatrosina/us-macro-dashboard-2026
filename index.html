Stagflation watch: the 2026 energy shock

Inflation has reaccelerated for three straight months as the war over Iran's nuclear program pushes oil and gasoline prices higher — even as the labor market, manufacturing and services activity have so far held up. This is the data behind the toughest call of new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's tenure.

Where the economy sits: growth vs. inflation

Signature view

A simple two-axis read on the business cycle: real GDP growth against core inflation, relative to roughly trend growth (~2%) and the Fed's 2% target. The economy isn't in a stagflation today — but the direction of travel matters more than the level.

Reflation, not (yet) stagflation

Both readings sit on the warm side of the chart: growth has stayed close to trend while inflation has moved further from target — a reflationary shock rather than a textbook stagflation. The risk case is the next two quarters: Michigan sentiment just hit a record low and the labor market has stopped adding jobs at its 2024 pace. If growth downshifts while energy-driven inflation stays sticky, the dot moves left, into the stagflation quadrant.

Prices & policy

The headline-core gap is the story: energy is doing most of the damage, but core inflation is drifting up too — enough that the Fed flipped from signalling cuts to signalling a possible hike.

Activity holds up — confidence doesn't

Factories and services firms are still expanding at the fastest clip since 2022. Households feel the opposite: sentiment just posted its lowest reading on record.

About this project

An independent macro-data dashboard tracking the U.S. business cycle through the 2026 energy shock. Built to demonstrate applied economic analysis: sourcing primary data (BLS, BEA, Federal Reserve, ISM), framing it through a growth/inflation lens, and communicating it for a non-specialist audience.

Data sources

  • U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics — CPI & employment situation
  • U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis — GDP, third estimate
  • Federal Reserve — FOMC statements & SEP (Jun 17, 2026)
  • Institute for Supply Management — Manufacturing PMI
  • University of Michigan — Surveys of Consumers

Methodology notes

Fed funds path before Dec 2025 is reconstructed from publicly reported cuts, not an official series. The "latest" quadrant point uses ISM's own PMI-to-GDP read-through as a growth proxy, since Q2 GDP is not released until Jul 30, 2026.
